Synthesis

What the trajectory as a whole argues, contributes, and leaves open

Four AT-LABs. Three Boundary Concepts. One EMI framework. The synthesis asks: what does this trajectory add up to, and what does it prove?

The Nested Structure of the Argument

The three layers of contribution are nested, not parallel. AT-LABs generate the evidence—they are the research environments from which all findings emerge. Boundary Concepts provide the middle layer—a comparative vocabulary that makes cross-lab analysis possible without flattening the differences between contexts. EMI operates above both—a framework for positioning the whole trajectory's contribution within the broader landscape of artistic research in transdisciplinary collaboration.

The nesting is important because it determines the logic of the argument. You cannot evaluate Boundary Concepts without first understanding the AT-LAB methodology that generated them. You cannot evaluate EMI without understanding both the AT-LABs and the conceptual middle layer. The argument is cumulative; it does not work in fragments.

Layer 1 · Methodology

AT-LABs

Artistic research structured as iterative, context-sensitive, comparable inquiry. The contribution is formal: a research form that preserves situated character while enabling systematic reflection across contexts.
Layer 2 · Comparative

Boundary Concepts

Conceptual Clarity, Perceived Agency, Transferability. Not metrics—diagnostic lenses. The contribution is analytical: a middle layer that enables comparison without universalisation.
Layer 3 · Framework

EMI

Epistemic Mediation Indicators. A practitioner-facing vocabulary for positioning artistic research's epistemic contribution within transdisciplinary collaboration. The contribution is political: making the value of artistic research legible on its own terms.

Five Innovation Claims

The following five claims articulate what this PD contributes to the field. They are stated directly and without hedging:

AT-LABs as repeatable, context-sensitive artistic research methodology

Artistic research can be structurally iterative—not in the sense of applying the same method to different problems, but in the deeper sense of learning across iterations, accumulating knowledge through reflection and feed-forward, and developing a methodology that becomes more precise with each application. This PD demonstrates that claim across four radically different contexts.

Literacy as staged condition rather than instructional goal

AI and data literacy need not be transmitted through instruction. They can emerge through designed encounter—sensory, relational, aesthetic, collective. The shift from 'how do we teach AI literacy?' to 'what conditions allow AI literacy to emerge?' is not merely semantic; it restructures the entire research and design problem.

Boundary Concepts as a middle layer between local specificity and cross-context comparison

Neither fully universal nor purely situated, Boundary Concepts occupy a methodologically productive middle position. They make comparison possible without requiring identical conditions. They are robust without being reductive. Their emergence from practice—rather than from theory—is an argument about where methodological innovation in artistic research comes from.

ATMOS_FERA as translational modelling

An immersive artistic performance can function as an epistemic instrument—one that makes abstract systems perceptible, that positions perception as critique, that produces knowledge through embodied collective encounter. ATMOS_FERA does not illustrate this claim; it enacts it.

Artistic research as epistemic contribution in transdisciplinary settings

The EMI framework makes a structural claim: artistic research contributes to knowledge-making in ways that are specific, identifiable, and irreducible to impact metrics or output lists. The framework does not prove this claim—it provides a vocabulary for making it visible, examining it, and refining it across different collaborative contexts.

What the Trajectory Leaves Open

This PD does not close the question of AI and data literacy through art. It opens it more precisely. The four AT-LABs produced findings that are situated, partial, and context-dependent—which is the correct epistemological register for artistic research that takes its situatedness seriously.

What remains open: the conditions under which transferability becomes durable rather than fleeting; the relationship between individual and collective literacy formation; the specific ways EMI dimensions manifest differently in health, educational, civic, and artistic contexts; and the question of how the AT-LAB methodology translates to researchers other than the one who developed it.

These are not weaknesses to apologise for. They are the next set of research questions that this trajectory makes possible.

The artistic environment is not a vehicle for transmitting pre-existing knowledge. It is a condition for the emergence of knowledge that could not have been generated otherwise.
