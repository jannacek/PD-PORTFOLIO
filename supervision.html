Supervision

Supervisory committee, structure, and developmental arc of the trajectory

The supervisory committee for this PD was assembled around a specific transdisciplinary logic: one that could hold artistic research methodology, digital sovereignty theory, health-science domain expertise, and AI theory in productive tension. The four-person team reflects the actual structure of the work — not a generic committee, but a specific constellation of expertise configured around the questions this trajectory poses.

Supervisory Committee

Supervising Professor (Primary) · 20 days/year
Prof. Dr. Anke Coumans
Chair of Image in Context · Minerva Art Academy / Research Centre Art & Society · Hanze University of Applied Sciences, Groningen

Coumans has held the Chair of Image in Context at Minerva Art Academy since 2012, embedded in the Research Centre Art & Society at Hanze UAS. She obtained her PhD from Leiden University in 2010 with a dissertation on semiotics and the public image. Her research investigates how art functions as a catalyst within socio-political ecosystems, and has developed the concept of the "artistic attitude" as an epistemological stance — the capacity to see things differently within social and collaborative contexts. She co-leads the UAS Professional Doctorate in Arts + Creative programme and developed the Interrelational Art Practices (IRAP) master profile at the Frank Mohr Master Institute.

Why this matters to the trajectory: Coumans provides the primary intellectual scaffolding for the AT-LAB methodology's grounding in participatory, practice-based inquiry. Her concept of the artistic attitude directly shapes how the AT-LABs were designed as epistemically generative environments — not controlled experiments, but structured encounters between artistic practice and community. She is the daily point of contact, bridges the PD to the Research Group Art & Society and the Healthy Living as a Service consortium, and coordinates the supervisory team across four years.
Supervising Professor (Second) · 5 days/year
Prof. Dr. Bianca Herlo
Professor of Design · Lucerne University of Applied Sciences and Arts (since 2024) · formerly Research Group Leader, Weizenbaum Institute / UdK Berlin Design Research Lab

Herlo's research sits at the intersection of social design, digital sovereignty, and design justice. At UdK Berlin's Design Research Lab and the Weizenbaum Institute for the Networked Society, she led research on inequality and digital participation in networked societies. She is Chairperson of the German Society for Design Theory and Research (DGTF) and co-founder of the international Social Design Network. Since September 2024, she holds a professorship at Lucerne University of Applied Sciences and Arts, where her research group focuses on "Design, Diversity and New Commons."

Why this matters to the trajectory: Herlo provides the critical grounding in digital sovereignty that runs through the PD's conceptual spine — from its original title ("Lifestyle and Digital Sovereignty") through to the empowerment and agency dimensions of the Boundary Concepts. Her expertise challenges the AT-LABs to go beyond literacy-as-knowledge toward literacy-as-agency. Her connection to the Weizenbaum Institute was also the institutional basis for the planned exchange period in the activity plan. She sharpens the ethical and socio-political stakes of every intervention, particularly at the intersection of community data, AI systems, and public participation.
Supervising Professional · 5 days/year
Prof. Dr. Claudine Lamoth
Professor of Movement Analysis & Smart Technology in Aging · UMCG (University Medical Centre Groningen) · Scientific Director, HTRIC (Health Technology Research Innovation Cluster, since 2023)

Lamoth is a full professor at UMCG's Faculty of Medical Sciences, where her research investigates gait, postural control, and physical activity in age- and lifestyle-related diseases through wearable sensor technology and AI/data science. Her expertise spans clinical coordination dynamics, games for health, exergaming, and sensor-mediated movement analysis. She serves as Scientific Director of HTRIC and section editor for the Journal of Human Movement Science.

Why this matters to the trajectory: Lamoth represents the health-science anchor of the PD's formative context. The Healthy Living as a Service (HLaS) consortium — within which AT-LAB I was designed and conducted — is co-directed from the UMCG side by her group. Her expertise in sensor-mediated body awareness and wearable technology is directly embedded in AT-LAB I's biosensor-based installation. She ensures the artistic intervention remains grounded in its health-research context rather than becoming aesthetically detached. Her presence on the committee is evidence of the PD's genuinely transdisciplinary character.
Supervising Professional · 5 days/year
Dr. Michel van Dartel
Research Professor of Situated Art, Design and Technology · CARADT (Centre of Applied Research for Art, Design and Technology, Avans University) · former Director, V2_Lab for the Unstable Media, Rotterdam (2005–2024)

Van Dartel holds an MSc in cognitive psychology and a PhD in artificial intelligence, both from Maastricht University. After his doctorate, he joined V2_Lab for the Unstable Media in Rotterdam — the internationally prominent interdisciplinary centre for art and media technology — where he served as Director until November 2024. He now holds a tenured Research Professorship at CARADT/Avans, focused on Situated Art, Design and Technology, and works as an independent curator and advisor to art academies and cultural funding bodies.

Why this matters to the trajectory: Van Dartel provides the deep theoretical grounding in artificial intelligence that the PD's research questions demand. His background in cognitive psychology and AI theory — combined with his curatorial and network role at V2_ — means he can challenge both the conceptual rigour of how AI is framed in the AT-LABs and open institutional doors across the Dutch and European media art ecology. His V2_ AI Cluster research group was a key site for situating the PD's research claims. His own trajectory — from hard AI science to artistic curation — is a professional mirror of the PD's central question: what kind of knowledge does artistic practice with AI produce?

Supervisory Structure

The Training and Supervision Plan (TSP) was signed on 8 December 2022 and established the formal supervisory architecture for the trajectory. The committee operates on a distributed time allocation: the primary supervisor (Coumans) allocates 20 days per year to individual meetings, oversight, and coordination; the three co-supervisors each allocate 5 days per year, concentrating their input at key conceptual junctures and methodological crossroads.

Supervision is structured around fortnightly individual meetings (minimum) with the primary supervisor, combined with full committee meetings at least three times per year. These committee meetings are deliberately timed to coincide with major project milestones: the completion of each AT-LAB, the consolidation of boundary concepts, and the synthesis phases. Between full meetings, written feedback and asynchronous collaboration occur via shared project documentation.

The candidate's own time is distributed across research (75%), teaching/curriculum development (12.5%), and training/professional development (12.5%), as per the PD charter. This allocation reflects the position that research and teaching are inseparable within artistic practice, and that currency in emerging fields (AI theory, digital sovereignty, participatory design) requires ongoing engagement with training environments.

This supervisory model is grounded in the Leiden University Golden Rules for PhD Supervision, adapted for the Professional Doctorate context where practice-based research requires different rhythms and evidence forms than traditional doctoral science.

Key Supervisory Moments

Go/No-Go Assessment · 15 April 2024

The 9-month assessment confirmed the trajectory direction and integration of all four PD roles across completed work. Evidence reviewed included the AT-LAB I community installation at Stichting Landgoed de Camping, 't Zandt (June 2023, approximately 50 participants); spatial sound training at 4DSOUND System in Stone Nest, London (15–19 May 2023); AI course development at Frank Mohr Institute (1 EC, June 2023); the PELE Iceland expedition conducting lava tube fieldwork in collaboration with Columbia University, NYU, Akureyri University, Utrecht University, and University of Tartu; and dissemination activities including European Researchers' Night at Groningen Forum and the Healthy Living as a Service Symposium in Roden. The Second PD Symposium in Utrecht demonstrated emerging coherence across the cohort. The committee confirmed the trajectory without adjustment to core direction. Outcome: Go — full continuation.

Sharpening the Research Frame · Post AT-LAB I

The most significant conceptual tightening of the trajectory emerged from supervisory dialogue following AT-LAB I. The original "wellbeing" frame — broad enough to accommodate many interpretations — was refocused toward AI and data literacy specifically. This was not a change of direction but a necessary sharpening: one that made the subsequent AT-LABs more precise, made the Boundary Concepts more tractable as comparative measures, and made the final EMI framework possible as a practitioner-facing tool rather than a generic reflection on health. This clarification was crucial to the coherence of the research claim and to the integrity of the Four Roles model. The supervisory committee pushed back against diffuseness and demanded that the PD articulate its distinctive epistemic contribution.

ATMOS_FERA as AT-LAB IV · Year 3

The decision to designate ATMOS_FERA as AT-LAB IV — rather than as a dissemination output or artistic by-product of the PD — was reached in supervisory dialogue and represents a key moment of methodological consolidation. It required arguing, collectively, that a live immersive performance could function as a research environment in the same methodological sense as a community workshop or classroom. The argument rested on the AT-LAB's structural definition: design → intervention → measure → reflection → feed-forward. ATMOS_FERA satisfies all five: its epistemic claim is tested in performance, measurable through the internal coherence of its dramaturgical structure, and feeds forward into the ISEA 2026 paper. This moment affirmed that the research is fundamentally about what happens in the room — the gathering, the bodies, the sensor data, the sound — and that performance is a legitimate site of knowledge production.
