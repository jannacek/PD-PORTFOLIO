Boundary Concepts

Three diagnostic lenses that emerged from AT-LAB practice and enable cross-lab comparison: Conceptual Clarity · Perceived Agency · Transferability

Three concepts emerged from within the iterative practice and were formally refined across all four AT-LABs. They function as lightweight, relational lenses: tools for asking what happened inside each AT-LAB, and for noticing what carried across contexts. They are not metrics or KPIs. They are not outcomes to be achieved. They describe conditions for intelligibility.

Conceptual Clarity
naming the experience
→
Perceived Agency

→
Transferability

Together, these three form a minimal epistemic sequence: clarity → agency → transfer. They describe not what was learned but the conditions under which learning became possible.

The Three Concepts

Boundary Concept 01

Conceptual Clarity

Conceptual clarity does not mean correct understanding. It names a shift in how participants articulate what they think a system is doing, and how they locate themselves in relation to it. In AT-LAB I, the biosensor installation revealed a gap between using wearable technology and understanding it, making self-tracking and data ownership newly thinkable. In AT-LAB II, this became a central finding: students showed conceptual ambiguity about AI, and “begin with conceptual clarification” emerged as a core pedagogical parameter. Conceptual clarity thus moved from an emergent issue in the first lab to a refined comparative lens in the second. It describes the first condition of intelligibility in the trajectory: the emergence of situated language before agency or transfer can follow.

Boundary Concept 02

Perceived Agency

Perceived agency is not mastery or control. It is the felt possibility of response: the sense that one’s actions, interpretations, or refusals matter within a mediated environment. In AT-LAB II, agency appeared as interpretive authority: students’ sense of their right to define what AI meant for their own artistic practice, rather than simply inherit dominant framings of it. In AT-LAB III, this became explicitly collective: participants felt more able to question AI-generated imagery when doing so with others, and the live voting structure made group position visible in real time. In AT-LAB IV, agency shifts again. ATMOS_FERA does not ask the audience to control the work, but to navigate it through presence, movement, and attention within an immersive environment with no fixed point of view. Perceived agency therefore emerged most clearly in AT-LAB II, expanded in AT-LAB III, and was tested at atmospheric scale in AT-LAB IV. It describes a condition in which participants are not passive recipients of a system, but experience themselves as able to respond within it.

Boundary Concept 03

Transferability

Transferability is not scalability. It does not ask whether the same insight applies everywhere, nor whether it can be expanded without change. It asks what carries over: whether something produced in one AT-LAB reappears elsewhere — in language, practice, method, or imagination — transformed but still recognisable. In this sense, transferability is the boundary concept that makes polylocality workable in the trajectory. Each AT-LAB operates as a distinct local world, with its own participants, temporalities, affordances, and forms of meaning-making; transferability asks whether an insight can move between these worlds without being flattened into a universal model. In AT-LAB II, this became visible when students applied course insights to their own artistic projects and daily encounters with AI. In AT-LAB III, transferability became explicit at the level of civic method: the Promptare workshop, the Semiotic Protocol card set, and the facilitation framework were all developed to travel into other participatory settings through an open toolkit logic. Transferability therefore names the continued life of an insight across different local conditions. The point at which something generated in one context begins to resonate in another.

How They Work Together

The sequence—clarity before agency, agency before transferability—is not incidental. It reflects a pedagogical and epistemic logic: participants need language before they can feel response is possible; they need felt agency before insight can travel. This does not mean the sequence is always clean or linear. In ATMOS_FERA, all three conditions operated simultaneously and recursively. But the sequence describes what to look for when asking whether an AT-LAB worked.

That the three concepts function across four radically different contexts—a community health installation, a master's-level course, an international civic workshop, a large-scale performance—is itself the finding. They are robust without being universal. They describe conditions that matter differently depending on context, and they resist being reduced to a score.

What These Concepts Are Not

Boundary Concepts are not the same as EMI (Epistemic Mediation Indicators). Boundary Concepts operate within the AT-LAB cycle—they are the lenses used to analyse what happened inside each intervention. EMI operates above it—it is a framework for positioning the whole trajectory's contribution within transdisciplinary collaboration. The two work at different levels and should not be conflated.

Boundary Concepts are also not a measurement system. They do not produce scores, rankings, or before/after comparisons. They are diagnostic prompts: after each AT-LAB, the question is not 'Did we achieve X?' but 'What conditions for clarity, agency, and transfer did this environment create—and for whom, and how?' The answer is always interpreted, situated, and partial. That is not a weakness. That is the epistemic register in which this research operates.
