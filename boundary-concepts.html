Boundary Concepts

Three diagnostic lenses that emerged from AT-LAB practice and enable cross-lab comparison: Conceptual Clarity · Perceived Agency · Transferability

Three concepts emerged from within the iterative practice and were formally refined across all four AT-LABs. They function as lightweight, relational lenses: tools for asking what happened inside each AT-LAB, and for noticing what carried across contexts. They are not metrics or KPIs. They are not outcomes to be achieved. They describe conditions for intelligibility.

Conceptual Clarity
naming the experience
→
Perceived Agency
felt possibility of response
→
Transferability
insight in motion

Together, these three form a minimal epistemic sequence: clarity → agency → transfer. They describe not what was learned but the conditions under which learning became possible.

The Three Concepts

Boundary Concept 01

Conceptual Clarity

Conceptual clarity does not mean correct understanding. It means a shift in how people name and situate their experience—a movement from vague familiarity to located language. In AT-LAB I, participants gradually developed language for what the biosensors were doing; they began calling it 'data about me' rather than 'the machine.' In AT-LAB II, students moved from 'AI is a tool' to 'AI is a set of assumptions'—a conceptual shift with real artistic implications. Did engagement produce language? That is the question clarity asks. Conceptual Clarity emerged as the foundational condition from AT-LAB I's work with community participants navigating health data, and it proved transferable across every subsequent context because it describes a universal epistemic need: the necessity of naming before agency becomes possible.

Boundary Concept 02

Perceived Agency

Perceived agency is not mastery or control. It is felt possibility of response—the sense that one's actions, interpretations, or refusals have consequences within the environment. In AT-LAB III, participants at the Promptare! workshop who initially felt overwhelmed by the AI interface gradually developed a relationship with the tool as something they could negotiate with rather than be subject to. In AT-LAB IV, the performance was designed so that the audience's collective presence—their breathing, movement, attention—shaped the sound environment in real time. Did the environment enable felt capacity to act? That is the question agency asks. Perceived Agency emerged as essential through AT-LAB II's teaching context, where students needed to move beyond passive consumption of AI concepts to active imagination of their own artistic relationships with the technology. It operates at every scale, from intimate one-to-one interactions to collective audience presence.

Boundary Concept 03

Transferability

Transferability is not scalability. It is not about whether the same insights apply to everyone, or whether they can be implemented at greater scale without modification. It is about whether an insight travels: whether understanding produced in one context shows up—transformed but recognisable—in a different one. A student from AT-LAB II who arrived uncertain about what AI was left articulating a specific critique of training data extraction in their artistic work. A civic participant from AT-LAB III used a phrase from the Semiotic Protocol game to describe a planning decision six months later. Did insight move beyond the encounter that produced it? That is the question transferability asks. Transferability became measurable through AT-LAB III's civic context, where we could track whether participants applied learning from the workshops to their ongoing practice in urban planning and community engagement. The third Boundary Concept is not about universal application but about the movement of thought across different contexts and timescales.

How They Work Together

The sequence—clarity before agency, agency before transferability—is not incidental. It reflects a pedagogical and epistemic logic: participants need language before they can feel response is possible; they need felt agency before insight can travel. This does not mean the sequence is always clean or linear. In ATMOS_FERA, all three conditions operated simultaneously and recursively. But the sequence describes what to look for when asking whether an AT-LAB worked.

That the three concepts function across four radically different contexts—a community health installation, a master's-level course, an international civic workshop, a large-scale performance—is itself the finding. They are robust without being universal. They describe conditions that matter differently depending on context, and they resist being reduced to a score.

What These Concepts Are Not

Boundary Concepts are not the same as EMI (Epistemic Mediation Indicators). Boundary Concepts operate within the AT-LAB cycle—they are the lenses used to analyse what happened inside each intervention. EMI operates above it—it is a framework for positioning the whole trajectory's contribution within transdisciplinary collaboration. The two work at different levels and should not be conflated.

Boundary Concepts are also not a measurement system. They do not produce scores, rankings, or before/after comparisons. They are diagnostic prompts: after each AT-LAB, the question is not 'Did we achieve X?' but 'What conditions for clarity, agency, and transfer did this environment create—and for whom, and how?' The answer is always interpreted, situated, and partial. That is not a weakness. That is the epistemic register in which this research operates.
