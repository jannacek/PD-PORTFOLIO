05 Trajectory · AT-LAB I
AT-LAB I · Health · Digital Sovereignty · Body

AT-LAB I —
Health

A biosensor-mediated participatory installation exploring digital health literacy and bodily agency in the HLaS context, 't Zandt, June 2023

Healthy Living as a Service ~50 Participants June 2–4, 2023 Wearable Biosensors Sound · Light · Movement

AT-LAB I was designed as both a practice-based research intervention and a community experience: an immersive audiovisual environment in which participants' own bodily data — heartbeat, movement, breath — directly controlled the light, sound, and spatial behaviour of the installation. It was the first iteration of the AT-LAB methodology, conducted within the Healthy Living as a Service (HLaS) consortium at Stichting Landgoed de Camping, 't Zandt.

The research question driving this intervention was how participatory artistic experiences and practices in technology could improve digital health literacy — specifically around wearable self-tracking, sensor interaction, and questions of agency and digital sovereignty in health-related contexts. The choice of wearable biosensors was not arbitrary: it placed the participant's body at the centre of both the aesthetic and the epistemic experience.

Around 50 participants entered the custom-built space over three days. Movement sensors placed on their arms and heartbeat sensors directly influenced the parameters of light, visuals, and spatial sound — transforming the installation into a live biometric instrument. Qualitative surveys and post-experience interviews documented participants' responses and their evolving relationship to health technology.

The Environment

Custom-built immersive environment at Stichting Landgoed de Camping. Participants entered individually or in small groups. The space was mapped through sensor technology and artistically intervened through sound, light, and choreographic direction. Movement sensors on arms combined with heartbeat sensors to create direct influence on light intensity, visual parameters, and spatial sound. Participants became both audience and instrument, their bodies literally playing the environment.

Research Evidence

Quantitative & Qualitative Data
  • Anonymised surveys with 50 participants
  • Qualitative interviews post-experience
  • Documentation of installation behaviour and participant interaction
  • Survey visualisations: perceived body awareness, digital sovereignty attitudes, technological mediation
  • Coding of participant reflections using inductive scheme
Key Findings
  • Participants engaged strongly with the biosensor interface
  • High frequency of surprise and bodily attentiveness
  • Desire for more control over own data: literacy emerged as empowerment, not just knowledge
  • Ambivalence about self-tracking: participants understood the technology but questioned its ownership structures
  • "Wellbeing" framing was too broad — outcome: shift toward AI and data literacy as specific focus

Boundary Concepts in AT-LAB I

The three Boundary Concepts were not pre-set at this stage — they emerged from analysis of AT-LAB I data and became the comparative measures applied across subsequent labs.

Conceptual Clarity
AT-LAB I status: Emergent. Most participants had limited prior framing of health wearables as data-generating devices. The installation surfaced the gap between using technology and understanding it. Conceptual clarity was low at entry, substantially higher post-experience — suggesting that embodied encounter is a viable route to conceptual understanding.
Perceived Agency
AT-LAB I status: Central finding. The desire for more control over one's own data was the strongest and most consistent participant response. Agency was not merely theoretical — participants physically experienced the connection between their body and the system, then asked: who else has access to this? This finding drove the agency dimension of Boundary Concepts.
Transferability
AT-LAB I status: Formative. Participants connected the AT-LAB experience to daily health tracking (Fitbits, phone sensors) spontaneously. Transfer from the artistic context to everyday life contexts was high — confirming that the AT-LAB environment generates insights that participants carry beyond it.

Feed-Forward to AT-LAB II

The central lesson of AT-LAB I — that literacy is not just knowledge but empowerment, and that the "wellbeing" frame was too diffuse — fed directly into AT-LAB II's design. The move to an educational context allowed the same questions to be sharpened: who defines what AI is? How does conceptual clarity about AI emerge through practice? What does the classroom teach us about collective sense-making that a community installation cannot? AT-LAB II was designed as an iterative response to these open questions.
