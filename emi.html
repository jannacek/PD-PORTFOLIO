EMI Framework

Six dimensions for practitioner-facing evaluation of epistemic mediation in AI literacy environments

EMI is a practitioner-facing qualitative framework for making the epistemic contribution of artistic research legible within transdisciplinary collaboration. It is not a metric system, a quality standard, or an impact checklist. It is a vocabulary: a set of analytical dimensions that help artist-researchers position their work, reflect on its epistemic function, and articulate its contribution in terms that are accessible to collaborators from other disciplines.

The problem EMI addresses is structural. Existing evaluation frameworks for artistic research—whether output-based, peer-review-driven, or impact-oriented—were not built to capture how artistic research reorganises knowledge-making from within a collaboration. EMI is a provisional attempt to name what those frameworks miss.

The Six Dimensions

01
Problem Reconfiguration

Did artistic work change how the question was framed?

Not whether art 'solved' the research problem, but whether it changed what the problem was understood to be. In the HLaS context, the initial research question was about digital health literacy as information acquisition. The artistic intervention shifted it toward literacy as embodied, relational, and affective—a reframing with significant consequences for method and design.

02
Knowledge Integration

What types of knowledge were brought into contact?

Artistic research typically brings embodied, aesthetic, speculative, and experiential knowledge into contact with empirical, clinical, social-scientific, or technical knowledge. The question is not whether these integrate smoothly—they often do not—but what becomes thinkable or perceptible through their encounter that was not thinkable before.

03
Epistemic Position & Authority

Did artistic intervention alter who could define relevance?

In collaborative research, authority over what counts as relevant knowledge, valid evidence, or meaningful outcome is rarely equal. Artistic intervention can—deliberately or unexpectedly—shift who has voice, whose experience is treated as data, and whose interpretation of events counts. This dimension tracks those shifts in epistemic authority.

04
Experiential Reorientation

Did collaborators or participants sense the issue differently?

One of the distinctive contributions of artistic research is the capacity to make abstract systems, data structures, or technological processes perceptible—not just thinkable. ATMOS_FERA transforms NASA atmospheric data into a sensory environment. The question is not whether participants 'learned' facts, but whether they experienced the problem space in a new register.

05
Evaluation Friction

Where did existing criteria fail to capture what the work was doing?

Every instance where standard evaluation criteria prove inadequate—where reviewers struggle to categorise, where funders can't find the right box, where output-based metrics feel plainly beside the point—is a trace of epistemic contribution. This dimension treats friction with existing frameworks not as a problem to be solved but as a diagnostic signal worth mapping.

06
Context & Stage Sensitivity

When and under what conditions did contribution become visible?

Artistic contributions to collaborative research are rarely immediate or straightforwardly legible. They often become visible retrospectively, at oblique angles, or in the practice of other collaborators. This dimension asks: at what stage of the collaboration did the artistic contribution register? Under what conditions—of trust, time, shared vocabulary, institutional openness—did it become thinkable?

EMI in This Trajectory

EMI was developed from two embedded cases: the HLaS Work Package 2 (AT-LAB I and the broader consortium work) and ATMOS_FERA (AT-LAB IV). These are not proof of the framework's validity—they are the probes from which its dimensions were derived. The framework is provisional and exploratory; it makes no claim to comprehensiveness.

Across the four AT-LABs, the most consistently active dimensions were Problem Reconfiguration (each lab shifted how the literacy question was understood), Experiential Reorientation (the core wager of participatory artistic research), and Evaluation Friction (persistent across all contexts). Epistemic Position & Authority was most visible in AT-LAB III, where the workshop structure explicitly redistributed authority over urban meaning-making.

Positioning EMI

EMI positions itself in relation to existing frameworks—the S+T+ARTS Prize model, the Flemish FRIS evaluation system, the transdisciplinary collaboration literature (Nowotny, Klein, Gibbons)—while arguing that none of these is practitioner-facing in the sense required. They are institutional evaluation tools, not reflective instruments for artist-researchers working inside collaboration.

The distinction matters because the intended user is not the funding panel or the review committee. It is the artist-researcher asking: what am I doing here, epistemically? What am I contributing that my collaborators cannot? Where is the real friction? EMI is a vocabulary for answering those questions—not a score, not a rubric, not a proof.
