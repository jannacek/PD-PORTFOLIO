AT-LAB IV · Atmosphere · Perception · Epistemic Practice

AT-LAB IV —
ATMOS_FERA

A live immersive audiovisual performance constructing a sequence of planetary atmospheres through light, fog, and spatial sound — presence as a mode of inquiry

ADE 2025 · ISO Amsterdam 45–55 min · Live Performance ISEA 2026 · Accepted NASA PSG Data HoloGauze · KIKUNO · Fog

Why This Is AT-LAB IV

ATMOS_FERA is not a dissemination output. It is a research environment. The question it poses is whether an immersive performance can function as an epistemic instrument — whether physical engagement with composed atmospheric conditions can constitute a form of knowing.

The designation of ATMOS_FERA as AT-LAB IV required a methodological argument — one developed in supervisory dialogue and articulated in the accepted ISEA 2026 paper. The AT-LAB definition requires: design → intervention → measure → reflection → feed-forward. ATMOS_FERA satisfies all five. The seven-act dramaturgical structure is the design. The live performance is the intervention. The measure is the internal coherence of the atmospheric argument: do the physically computed conditions produce distinct, legible perceptual regimes? The ISEA 2026 paper is the reflection. The feed-forward is the PD's concluding claim: that environmental literacy can be achieved through perceptual encounter, not only through representation or explanation.

This is also where the PD's research logic is most compressed. Every AT-LAB has asked what an artistic environment can make legible. AT-LAB IV asks whether the body can become an instrument of planetary inference.

Seven Acts: Atmospheric Sequence

v.1 · ISO Amsterdam · ADE 2025 · 45–55 min · no fixed point of view

I
Act I — Takeoff
Departure from the terrestrial. Pressure, turbulence, and the dissolution of familiar perceptual anchors. The body leaves behind its habitual relationship to light and scale. The work begins not with a planet but with the act of leaving one.
II
Act II — Venus
Dense, diffuse, yellow-white. Venus reflects nearly all incoming solar radiation through a thick cloud deck of sulphuric acid. The PSG-derived light is bright but structureless — no shadows, no horizon distinction. Fog fills the space. The atmosphere is total, not framed.
III
Act III — Mars
Rust-ochre, attenuated, vast. The Martian atmosphere is thin — the light arrives with low scattering, producing dusty, desaturated tones. Spatial sound opens into distance. The body is exposed. Mars is the act of extended, quiet duration.
IV
Act IV — Neptune
Deep blue, cold, remote. Neptune's atmosphere of hydrogen, helium, and methane produces a striking absorption signature in the red spectrum, leaving a saturated, luminous blue. The PSG-derived palette is among the most chromatically pure in the sequence. Spatial sound becomes ambient pressure.
V
Act V — Jupiter
Turbulent, banded, complex. Jupiter's upper atmosphere shows rapid chromatic variation across cloud layers — ammonia ice, ammonium hydrosulphide, water vapour. The GLSL shader system renders dynamic light shifts across the HoloGauze membrane. The audience moves within a system that is itself moving.
VI
Act VI — Return to Earth
Re-entry. The familiar atmosphere arrives differently after the sequence that preceded it. Blue sky re-read through CIE colorimetry — physically identical to the planetary data sets, but suddenly legible as home. The body's atmospheric calibration has shifted.
VII
Act VII — Terrestrial Sunrise / Algorithmic Drift
The closing act begins as a physically grounded terrestrial sunrise — PSG-computed, CIE-translated, correct. It then drifts. AI-generated atmospheric imagery enters: aesthetically plausible, visually continuous with what preceded it, but physically ungrounded. The perceptual destabilisation is the research's sharpest moment — not as spectacle, but as epistemological test. The audience has been trained, across seven acts, to read atmosphere through physical evidence. The AI-generated sequence asks whether that training holds when the evidence is simulated.

Technical Architecture

Pipeline — v.1

NASA PSG spectral data
→ CIE colorimetry (XYZ → sRGB)
→ GLSL shaders (KodeLife)
→ HAP codec
→ MadMapper projection mapping
→ HoloGauze membrane (3 × 2 m)
→ KIKUNO spatial sound

Every colour in the installation derives from atmospheric physics, not artistic interpretation. The translation chain treats radiative transfer data as a behavioural constraint — it determines what the light can do, not what it should look like.

Setup · ISO Amsterdam
  • HoloGauze screen — 3 × 2 m semi-transparent membrane with volumetric fog projection
  • KIKUNO spatial sound — custom configuration for ISO main hall
  • Fog — volumetric, spatially distributed
  • NASA PSG (Planetary Spectrum Generator) · Villanueva et al. 2018
  • Duration: 45–55 min · no fixed point of view · durational structure
  • Audience free to stand, move, linger — spatial negotiation is constitutive

Research Contribution

Physical engagement with composed atmospheric conditions can constitute a form of knowing. These are not representations of planets — they are translational models. The difference is the research.

The accepted ISEA 2026 paper — "Atmospheric Enactment as Epistemic Practice" — articulates the theoretical argument: that planetary atmospheres, when constructed through physically grounded translational pipelines, can function as epistemic environments. The evidence the paper marshals is technical and dramaturgical: the pipeline architecture, the fidelity of the CIE colorimetry translation, the internal coherence of the seven-act sequence as a controlled series of perceptual conditions. The paper does not claim empirical audience findings — it claims a methodology and a theoretical position. That is what makes it appropriate for ISEA.

The research contribution is threefold: (1) a translational methodology — radiative transfer data treated as compositional constraint rather than representational source; (2) a dramaturgical argument — the sequence of acts as a structured perceptual curriculum; (3) a critical test — Act VII's drift from physical to AI-generated atmosphere as an epistemological probe, asking whether perceptual training built on physical evidence can detect its simulation.

Boundary Concepts at the Capstone

At AT-LAB IV, the Boundary Concepts are no longer operationalised through questionnaires or coded reflection. They are structurally enacted: clarity through immersion, agency through spatial negotiation, transferability as an open claim about what perceptual training does to a body after it leaves the room.

Conceptual clarity in ATMOS_FERA does not arise from explanation. It arises — if it arises — from the legibility of the perceptual conditions themselves. Whether an audience member can distinguish one atmospheric regime from another on the basis of physical evidence alone is the implicit question that the dramaturgical structure poses. Perceived agency is the freedom to navigate the space, to choose where and how to attend. Transferability is the most theoretically ambitious claim: that the shift in perceptual orientation produced by the sequence leaves the room with the audience. This is argued theoretically in the ISEA paper. It is not yet documented empirically, and the portfolio does not claim otherwise.

This is what makes AT-LAB IV the methodological capstone: not because it resolves the PD's questions, but because it stages them at their most precise.
