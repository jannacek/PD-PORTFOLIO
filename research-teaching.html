Research ↔ Teaching

How teaching shaped research and how research shaped teaching: a bidirectional account

The PD allocates 12.5% of time to teaching — but this account argues the relationship is not quantitative. Teaching at the Frank Mohr Institute was not a side contribution to the research; it was a primary site of inquiry. AT-LAB II was a course. The findings from AT-LAB II fed back into the course design. The boundary between teaching and researching dissolved at the most productive moments.

The Course as Research Site

The course "AI: In, Within, and Around Your Practice" was not designed as a vehicle for delivering pre-formed research findings. It was designed as a participatory research environment—an AT-LAB—in which the teaching process itself was the subject of investigation. The students were not research subjects in any conventional sense; they were active participants in a shared inquiry into what AI literacy means for emerging artists.

The research question—how do emerging artists define, engage with, and imagine AI, and what shifts when artistic practice becomes the site of literacy?—required a live teaching context to be answerable. It could not have been investigated through a literature review, a survey of existing practitioners, or an analysis of student work from courses designed by others. It required designed encounter, real pedagogical stakes, and a group of learners genuinely wrestling with the question.

How Teaching Shaped Research

Conceptual development
The three pedagogical parameters that crystallised from AT-LAB II—conceptual clarification, critical literacy throughout, curation as research—were not predicted from the theory. They emerged from the experience of teaching: from observing where students got stuck, where they opened up, and where the course design needed to change. They became the operational basis for the Boundary Concepts framework.
Methodological refinement
Teaching forced methodological precision. Designing the questionnaire for the course required articulating what exactly was being investigated. The act of curating the student exhibition required developing criteria for what "AI literacy in artistic practice" looks like. Both of these moved back into the research design for AT-LABs III and IV.
Empirical findings
The questionnaire data, the dialogue records, and the curatorial analysis all constitute empirical research materials. The gap between student AI use and artistic integration—the most significant finding of AT-LAB II—was not something that could have been hypothesised; it emerged from the course itself.
Peer-reviewed output
The P+ARTS 2025 publication "From Conceptual Ambiguity to Critical Engagement: AI Literacy as Artistic Research" is a direct output of AT-LAB II. It was written from the materials the course generated and submitted to a peer-reviewed venue. Teaching produced the primary research publication.

How Research Shaped Teaching

The relationship runs the other way too. The AT-LAB methodology—developed from AT-LAB I's findings about sensory encounter, conceptual scaffolding, and participatory design—directly informed the course structure. The three-way combination of disambiguation, experimentation, and critical literacy was not a pre-existing pedagogical framework imported into the research. It was a methodological decision: the course was designed as an AT-LAB, which meant it had a research question, research instruments (questionnaire, curatorial framework), and a feed-forward cycle built in.

Supervision meetings that addressed the research trajectory also addressed the teaching practice. The two were not discussed separately. Feedback on how the course was evolving shaped feedback on how the research was developing.
