AT-LAB II · Teaching · AI Literacy · Curation

AT-LAB II —
Teaching

AI literacy as artistic research through a higher-education course at the Frank Mohr Institute; from conceptual ambiguity to critical engagement

Frank Mohr Institute · Hanze UAS 2023–2024 MFA Cohort · 10 Students Curation as Research AI Literacy

AT-LAB II moved from a community health context to an educational one — from bodies interacting with wearable sensors to emerging artists grappling with what AI actually means to their practice. The shift was not merely contextual: it was a methodological refinement. Where AT-LAB I used embodied installation as the research medium, AT-LAB II used the course itself — the syllabus, the curation, the studio visit, the reflective assignment — as the AT-LAB environment.

The course "AI: In, Within, and Around Your Practice" was taught at the Frank Mohr Institute (FMI), Hanze UAS, to MFA students across the MADTech, Painting, MAP, and IRAP masters programmes. It began as a 1 EC module in June 2023 ("Discursive Context: AI in Artistic Practice") and was substantially expanded to 2 EC in April–June 2024, incorporating structured questionnaires, codified reflective assignments, and a curated exhibition as research output. The Encounters in Artistic Research showcase (September 2024) served as a dissemination and research format simultaneously.

The Course as Research Environment

Students presenting AI projects, Encounters in Artistic Research showcase, FMI · September 2024

Conceptual framing

Opening sessions on definitions of AI: students mapped their own understandings before any introduction of canonical definitions

Critical engagement

Studio visits, artistic analysis, mentoring sessions focused on AI as metaphor, tool, and epistemic condition

Curation as research

The final showcase was designed as a research act: selection criteria, curatorial statement, and reflective documentation were all required outputs

Research Evidence

Structured Questionnaire
10 MFA students across disciplines. Questions: definitions of AI, frequency of use (daily life vs. artistic practice), self-assessed literacy, desired future uses, views on AI futures. Key quantitative finding: high daily AI use, much lower integration in artistic practice — suggesting a deliberate separation between the personal and professional.
Qualitative Reflections
Coded student reflections using inductive scheme. Key qualitative finding: AI definitions were heterogeneous and often anchored in science fiction or popular media. Artistic AI use was aspirational but technically underconfident. The imagination exceeded the skill — a productive gap.
Curatorial Documentation
Encounters in Artistic Research 2024 (Minerva Art Academy, Groningen, September 2024). Exhibition of 10 student works responding to course questions. Curatorial framing: "AI as condition, not tool." Documentation used as research data: how do students externalise AI relationships through form, material, and concept?

Boundary Concepts in AT-LAB II

Conceptual Clarity (Refined)
The central finding of AT-LAB II. Students demonstrated conceptual ambiguity about AI — not ignorance, but heterogeneous and unstable conceptual frameworks. The pedagogical parameter "begin with conceptual clarification" emerged directly from this finding: clarity cannot be assumed; it must be actively constructed. This refined the Boundary Concept from AT-LAB I's emergent finding to a structured, measurable dimension.
Perceived Agency (Educational Register)
Agency in AT-LAB II took the form of interpretive authority — students' sense of their right to define what AI meant for their practice. The questionnaire revealed a pattern: students who used AI heavily in daily life often bracketed it from their artistic practice, suggesting an implicit sense that artistic agency required independence from algorithmic systems. Literacy as agency here was about reclaiming the right to define.
Transferability (High)
Students spontaneously applied AT-LAB II insights to their own artistic projects and to daily encounters with AI. Transfer was high and unsolicited — a strong indicator that the course achieved more than content transmission. The codebook developed here became a transferable instrument for subsequent AT-LABs.

Three Pedagogical Parameters

From AT-LAB II, three pedagogical parameters crystallised as transferable research outputs:

Begin with conceptual clarification

Do not assume shared AI definitions. Make definitional heterogeneity visible and generative.

Embed critical literacy throughout

AI literacy is not a module; it is a disposition cultivated across the arc of artistic learning.

Treat curation as research

The act of selecting, framing, and juxtaposing student work generates knowledge about AI relationships that individual reflection cannot.

Feed-Forward to AT-LAB III

AT-LAB II established the codebook and the three pedagogical parameters. But it also revealed a limit: literacy in an art school is necessarily self-selecting and context-specific. The question that fed into AT-LAB III was: what happens when AI literacy is pursued not in a classroom but in a civic public space, with citizens rather than artists, around questions of collective imagination and the future of their city? AT-LAB III was designed as a methodological answer.
